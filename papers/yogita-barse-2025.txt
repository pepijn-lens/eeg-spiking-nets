Abstract. Brain computing, or neuromorphic computing, is a
revolutionary field inspired by the human brain's parallel processing and
energy efficiency. Unlike traditional computers that separate memory and
processing, brain-inspired systems integrate these functions, enabling
faster, more efficient data handling.The core of this technology is the use
of artificial neural networks (ANNs) and specialized neuromorphic
hardware. ANNs mimic the brain's structure, while hardware like spiking
neural networks (SNNs) and memristors physically replicate the brain's
synaptic behavior. These systems excel at tasks like pattern recognition
and machine learning, where adaptability is key. However, the field faces
significant challenges. Developing scalable hardware that matches the
brain's complexity and creating effective software to train these systems
are major hurdles. Additionally, we need a deeper understanding of the
brain's own computational principles to unlock the full potential of this
technology.Despite these challenges, brain computing is poised to
transform various sectors, including artificial intelligence, robotics, and
healthcare, by creating a new generation of cognitive systems that can
learn and reason in a more human-like way.

Keywords: Brain Computing, EEG, Neuromorphic Computing,spiking
neural networks,Electrocorticography.

1. Introduction
The remarkable computational power and unparalleled energy efficiency of the human
brain have long served as a profound source of inspiration for computer scientists and
engineers. Unlike conventional computing systems based on the von Neumann architecture,
which suffers from the "memory wall" bottleneck due to the physical separation of
processing and memory units, the brain processes information in a massively parallel and
integrated manner [1]. This fundamental difference has motivated the emergence of brain
computing, a transformative field that seeks to emulate the brain's unique computational
principles to overcome the limitations of traditional computing paradigms.[2].

Brain computing, also referred to as neuromorphic computing, represents a significant
departure from the binary logic of digital computers.At its core, it is about designing and
building hardware and software systems that mimic the structure and function of biological
neural networks. This includes the use of artificial neurons and synapses, which are the
fundamental building blocks of the brain, to perform computations directly where data is
stored. This approach promises to deliver unprecedented improvements in power
efficiency, speed, and cognitive capabilities, particularly for tasks that the human brain
excels at, such as pattern recognition, sensory data processing, and complex decisionmaking in real-world environments[3-4].
This paper provides a comprehensive review of the current state of brain computing,
exploring the key technologies, applications, and challenges that define the field. We begin
by examining the core principles of neuromorphic hardware, including spiking neural
networks (SNNs) and novel memory technologies like memristors, which are designed to
replicate the brain's synaptic plasticity. We then delve into the diverse and impactful
applications of brain computing, ranging from medical devices and assistive technologies
for individuals with disabilities to advanced robotics and artificial intelligence systems.
Furthermore, we discuss the significant technical and ethical challenges that must be
addressed for the widespread adoption of this technology, including the need for new
programming paradigms, the development of scalable hardware, and the critical issues
surrounding privacy and security. By synthesizing current research and identifying future
directions, this paper aims to provide a clear and insightful overview of how brain
computing is poised to shape the future of computation and intelligence.[5].
This paper include Literature Review, including summary , Classification of Brain
Computing Interface, Phases of Brain Computing Interfaces., Neuromorphic Computing
and Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs).

BCI: Brain-Computer Interface (BCI)Fig. 1 shows it is a technology that enables
communication between the human brain and computers by utilizing brain activity. This
activity is most commonly measured using electroencephalography (EEG), a physiological
method in which electrodes are placed on the scalp surface to record brain signals.[14]. A
Brain-Computer Interface (BCI), also known as a brain–machine interface, enables direct
communication between the brain and external devices such as computers or robotic limbs.
Yike Sun [14–15]. Unlike traditional communication pathways that rely on vision,
movement, or speech, BCI bypasses these channels by directly linking the brain’s electrical
activity with the external world. This connection enhances human interaction with the
physical environment [16]. BCI serves as a non-muscular communication channel, allowing
the acquisition, processing, analysis, and translation of brain signals into commands that
can control external devices or applications.

2.1 BCI: Classification
2.1.1 Invasive:
An invasive Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) requires the surgical implantation of
electrodes into the brain tissue or onto its surface to capture neural activity, providing highquality signals. These BCIs are mainly intended for individuals with severe disabilities to
restore movement or communication abilities but come with higher risks, such as tissue
damage and infection. Examples include electrocorticography (ECoG) arrays and
intracortical microelectrode arrays[17].
Electrocorticography (ECoG):
Eectroencephalography as shown in Fig.2 is a types of monitoring that uses electrodes
placed directly on the brain’s exposed surface to recordelectrical activity ofcerebral cortex.
Whereas, a conventional electroencephalography (EEG) electrodes monitor activityfrom
outside the skull. ECoGcan be performed during the surgery in operating room
(intraoperative ECoG) or outside of surgery(extraoperativeECoG). Because a surgical
incision into the skull (craniotomy) is required toimplant the electrode grid, ECoG is an
invasive procedure[18].

Fig. 2 Intracranial electrode grid for electrocorticography.

2.1.2 Partially invasive:
Partially invasive brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) implant electrodes under the skull but
not directly into the brain. This approach, exemplified by electrocorticography (ECoG),
offers a balance between the high signal quality of invasive BCIs and the lower risk of noninvasive ones. Applications include neurorehabilitation and seizure prediction[19].
2.1.3 Non-invasive:
A non-invasive brain-computer interface (BCI) lets you control devices using brain signals
without surgery. It typically uses electroencephalography (EEG), which records electrical
activity via sensors placed on the scalp. Non-invasive BCIs are safe, affordable, and
accessible, but they offer lower signal accuracy compared to invasive methods [19].

preprocessing or enhancement, feature extraction, classification, and control interface[22].

3.2 Preprocessing: Signal processing in Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs) is a crucial
step that converts raw brain signals into meaningful and usable information. To ensure
signal clarity, quality, and reliability, this process involves multiple stages, including
preprocessing, feature extraction, and classification [24]. The first stage, preprocessing,
focuses on preparing the raw signals for further analysis by removing noise and
external interferences that may distort or weaken the data. Various filtering techniques
are applied, such as low-pass filters to remove high-frequency noise, high-pass filters
to eliminate low-frequency drift, and band-pass filters that allow only signals within a
specific frequency range to pass through. In addition, advanced methods like
Independent Component Analysis (ICA) are also used to separate and clean the signals
more effectively.
3.3 Feature Extraction: Feature extraction involves identifying and isolating distinctive
characteristics from the received signal. These features must closely reflect the user’s
intent, since much of the meaningful brain activity tends to be either transient or
oscillatory in nature[25].
3.4 Feature Translation: Once the features are extracted and classified, they are
processed through the feature translation algorithm. The primary role of this algorithm
is to transform the received signals into suitable commands that can be executed by the
output device[26].
3.5 Device Output: The output generated by the feature translation unit is delivered to
the designated device. These translated commands control external systems, enabling
functions such as letter selection, cursor movement, robotic arm manipulation, and
more. The resulting device actions provide feedback to the user, thereby completing
the control loop[27].

4 Neuromorphic Computing
Neuromorphic computing technologies, such as spiking neural networks (SNNs), replicate
brain-inspired neuron models and carry out computation through discrete spikes (Maass,
1997). Neuromorphic Computing replicate neural systems by constructing interconnected

propagation of spikes, enabling efficient parallel computation[28].

5 Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs)
Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) are a core component of neuromorphic computing. They
function based on discrete neural events known as spikes or action potentials. This eventdriven approach allows for efficient computation, contrasting with the continuous
processing found in traditional computing[29].

6 Conclusion
The field of Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs) has evolved from a theoretical idea into a
practical technology with significant clinical and social impacts. This progress is due to
major advancementsin both invasive and non-invasive techniques, supported by
improvements in signal processing and machine learning. Despite ongoing challenges, such
as the need to make non-invasive systems more reliable and user-friendly, current research
and development hold great promise for revolutionizing human-computer interaction,
neurorehabilitation, and restoring functions for people with disabilities.The future scope of
Brain Computer Interface (BCI) is vast and transformative pushing beyond current
limitations in clinical and consumer applications. Development is largely focused on three
interconnected areas: enhanced hardware, smarter algorithm, and widespread integration.
